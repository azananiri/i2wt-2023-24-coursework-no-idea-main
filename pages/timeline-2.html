Phoney War

The Phoney War, often called the drôle de guerre (French for "funny war") or sitzkrieg (German for "sitting war"), was a period of inaction on the Western Front during World War II. It began on September 1, 1939, when Germany invaded Poland, and lasted eight months, until May 10, 1940, when Germany launched an invasion of France and the Low Countries.

Blitzkrieg and the Chamberlain resigns

Blitzkrieg was a military tactic adopted by Nazi Germany during World War II to gain quick and decisive victories. It was based on surprise, mobility, shock, and local superiority of force. Blitzkrieg was extremely effective in the early years of the war, but it waned as allied troops devised efficient defences. British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain resigned after British soldiers failed to prevent Germany's invasion of Norway. The Norway Debate in the House of Commons had strongly criticised Chamberlain's handling of the war, and his government's majority had been significantly diminished. Chamberlain resigned as prime minister, seeing the importance of a national government supported by all major parties.

Battle of Britain

The Battle of Britain was a major air war fought between Britain's Royal Air Force (RAF) and Germany's Luftwaffe in the summer and autumn of 1940. Following Germany's defeat, the planned invasion of Britain was abandoned.

Tripartite Pact

The Tripartite Pact was a military agreement struck in 1940 by Germany, Italy, and Japan. The pact was formed to prevent the United States from joining the war in Europe and to divide the world among the Axis countries. It was one of the key reasons the United States entered World War II.

Hitler attacks Russia – Operation Barbarossa

Nazi Germany launched Operation Barbarossa, a large-scale invasion of the Soviet Union during World War II. The operation began on June 22, 1941, but was eventually unsuccessful for Germany. The Germans achieved early gains, but were eventually forced back by the Soviet forces. Operation Barbarossa was a watershed moment in the war, signalling the beginning of the end for Nazi Germany.

Pearl Harbor

The Imperial Japanese Navy conducted a surprise attack on the United States naval station in Honolulu, Hawaii, known as Pearl Harbor. It was a watershed moment in World War II because it brought the United States into the conflict and signalled the start of the Pacific War.

Battle of El Alamein

The Battle of El Alamein consisted of two key actions fought in the Egyptian desert between allied troops led by the British 8th Army and Axis forces headed by General Erwin Rommel's Afrika Korps. The first fight resulted in a stalemate, but the second, fought in October and November 1942, was a resounding allied triumph that represented a watershed moment in North Africa. It drove the Axis forces back and was a significant step in the Allies' assault for Germany.

D-Day in Normandy

D-Day, the codename for the Allied invasion of Normandy in World War II, was a watershed moment in the conflict. On June 6, 1944, more than 150,000 Allied troops landed on the beaches of Normandy, France, in a surprise attack against German forces. The invasion was successful, helping to liberate France and bringing the Allies closer to victory.

V2 Flying Bombs.

The V-2 was a long-range rocket produced by Germany during World War II. It was the world's first ballistic missile, capable of carrying a payload weighing 1,000 kg (2,200 lb) up to 200 miles. The V-2 was created as a terrorist weapon to demoralise populations. It was deployed against London and other allied cities, but it was inaccurate, resulting in more civilian casualties than military damage. The V-2 programme depleted German resources and eventually failed to achieve its goal of undermining allied morale.

Battle of the Bulge Begins

The Battle of the Bulge was a massive German operation in December 1944 that sought to divide allied forces and retake the port of Antwerp. Despite early advances, the Allies stopped the German offensive in January 1945. The fight was a costly defeat for Germany, signalling the end of their capacity to mount large offensive operations on the Western Front.

V.E. day

Victory in Europe Day, or V.E. Day, marked the end of World War II in Europe. On May 8, 1945, Germany surrendered to the Allied forces, and celebrations broke out across the continent. The war had claimed over 60 million lives, and V.E. Day was a time to remember the fallen and celebrate victory.
